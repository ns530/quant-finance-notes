The Distribution Trap That Kills Most Traders

Your risk model is probably built on a dangerous assumption. Here is the math that proves it — and Python output to show exactly how much it matters.

Nadun SulochanaAspiring Software Architect · BSc Cyber Security · SLTC Research University

Most finance models assume your returns follow a Normal distribution. That friendly, symmetric bell curve. Real markets crash far more often than it predicts. Here is the math — and the Python output — that proves it.

Why This Matters Before Anything Else

Before you build a trading strategy, backtest a signal, or size a position — you need to understand what distribution you are assuming your returns follow. Because that assumption is the foundation of every risk calculation you will ever make.

If the foundation is wrong, every number built on it is wrong too. And most people are using the wrong foundation.

The Normal distribution is not wrong because it is inaccurate. It is wrong because it makes crashes feel impossible — right until the moment they happen.

The 3 Distributions Every Trader Must Know

Distribution 1
Normal

The one every textbook teaches. Symmetric bell curve. Easy to work with. Assumes extreme moves are nearly impossible.

The Python Code — Run It Yourself

This code asks one question two ways: what is the worst loss you should expect on 1 in every 100 trading days? First using Normal. Then using the fat-tailed Student-t with df=4, which fits real stock returns well.

python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# Parameters — 0% mean, 1.5% daily volatility
mu, sigma = 0.0, 1.5

# Value at Risk at 99% confidence — Normal distribution
var_normal = stats.norm.ppf(0.01, mu, sigma)

# Value at Risk at 99% confidence — fat-tailed Student-t
var_t = stats.t.ppf(0.01, df=4, loc=mu, scale=sigma)

print(f"Normal VaR 99%:    {var_normal:.2f}%")
print(f"Fat-tail VaR 99%:  {var_t:.2f}%")

A 3-sigma move happens once every 370 days if you trust Normal. With fat tails it happens once every 25 days. That is not a theoretical distinction. That is a completely different risk regime.

What Should You Actually Use?

Quick mental math

Normal is fine. Easy, fast, good enough for rough estimates.

Serious risk calculations

Use Student-t with df between 3 and 6. Your VaR, position sizing, and stress tests will give honest numbers.

Modeling stock price levels

Use Lognormal. Prices cannot go below zero. This is why Black-Scholes uses it as its foundation.

Three things to remember from Day 1

The model you choose determines the risk you think you have. Choose the wrong model and your risk estimates are wrong before you place a single trade.

  • Normal distribution underestimates crash probability by roughly 15× compared to what markets actually deliver
  • Student-t with df=4 gives VaR estimates 61% worse than Normal — and that is the honest number
  • Always check the kurtosis of your return series before choosing a distribution. The data tells you what to use.
