Why Your Backtest is Lying to You

The Central Limit Theorem and Law of Large Numbers are the two most powerful ideas in all of statistics. They are also the two most misused — and misunderstanding them is exactly why most backtests fail in live trading.

Two theorems underpin almost every statistical claim in finance. The Central Limit Theorem explains why sample averages behave nicely even when individual returns are ugly. The Law of Large Numbers explains why you need far more trades than you think to trust your results.

The Law of Large Numbers — Convergence Takes Time

The Law of Large Numbers says something beautifully simple: as you collect more observations, your sample average converges to the true population mean. The more data you have, the closer you get to the truth.

Sounds obvious. But the implications for trading are brutal.

python
import numpy as np

# Simulate dice rolls — true mean = 3.5
np.random.seed(7)
rolls = np.random.randint(1, 7, 10000)

for n in [10, 100, 1000, 5000, 10000]:
    running_mean = rolls[:n].mean()
    error = abs(running_mean - 3.5)
    print(f"n={n:5d}: mean={running_mean:.4f}  error={error:.4f}")

Actual Output

The trading implication

With only 10 observations your estimate of the true mean is off by 0.4 — an 11% error. Most traders backtest with 50–200 trades and call that "statistically significant." With 100 trades your error is still 0.04. You need thousands of trades before your backtest truly converges to the real edge. This is why strategies that look great in backtesting fall apart in live trading.

The Central Limit Theorem — Why Averages Become Normal

The CLT is one of the most remarkable results in all of mathematics. It says: no matter what distribution your underlying data follows, the distribution of sample means will approach Normal as sample size increases.

It does not matter if your returns are skewed, exponential, uniform, or anything else. Average enough of them together and the distribution of those averages becomes bell-shaped. Every time.

Python Proof — Starting From a Skewed Distribution

python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(42)

# Exponential distribution — very skewed (skew ≈ 2.0)
population = np.random.exponential(scale=2, size=100000)
print(f"Population skew: {stats.skew(population):.4f}")
print()

# Draw sample means for increasing n
for n in [1, 5, 30, 100]:
    sample_means = [np.random.choice(population, n).mean()
                    for _ in range(10000)]
    sm = np.array(sample_means)
    print(f"n={n:3d}: mean={sm.mean():.4f}  std={sm.std():.4f}  "
          f"skew={stats.skew(sm):.4f}  kurt={stats.kurtosis(sm):.4f}")

The Standard Error Formula — How Precise Is Your Estimate?

The CLT gives us one of the most useful formulas in all of statistics. The standard error (SE) tells you exactly how much your sample mean varies from the true mean:

Standard Error of the Mean
SE = σ / √n
σ = population standard deviation  ·  n = sample size
Double your sample size → SE shrinks by factor of √2 ≈ 1.41
To halve your SE, you need 4× more data
▶ Standard error by sample size (σ = 1.986)
n= 1: theoretical SE = 1.9859 ← as uncertain as one data point
n= 5: theoretical SE = 0.8881
n= 30: theoretical SE = 0.3626
n=100: theoretical SE = 0.1986 ← 10× smaller than n=1

What This Means for Your Backtest

This is where the theory hits your trading account directly. Most traders backtest with too few trades and draw conclusions that are statistically meaningless.

❌ Dangerous backtest
50 trades · Sharpe ratio = 1.8
Win rate = 62%
"This strategy is profitable!"

With n=50 your standard error is enormous. The true Sharpe could be anywhere from 0.5 to 3.0. You cannot tell. This is not a strategy — it is noise with a good story.
✓ Trustworthy backtest
500+ trades · Sharpe ratio = 1.4
Win rate = 57%
"This strategy shows edge."

With n=500 your SE is small enough to trust. The CLT means your sample Sharpe is close to the true Sharpe. You can now make a reasonable decision about live deployment.
The minimum trades rule of thumb

For CLT to apply reliably: n ≥ 30 (mild non-normality in returns)
For meaningful Sharpe ratio: n ≥ 252 (one year of daily trades)
For genuine statistical significance: n ≥ 500 (serious quant standard)

If your backtest has fewer than 252 trades, treat the results as directional evidence only — not proof of edge.

The Resampling Solution — Bootstrap Your Backtest

Instead of trusting a single backtest run, use bootstrapping to estimate the distribution of possible outcomes. This applies CLT directly to your strategy evaluation:

python — bootstrap Sharpe ratio
import numpy as np

def sharpe(returns):
    return returns.mean() / returns.std() * np.sqrt(252)

def bootstrap_sharpe(returns, n_boot=10000):
    sharpes = [
        sharpe(np.random.choice(returns, len(returns), replace=True))
        for _ in range(n_boot)
    ]
    s = np.array(sharpes)
    print(f"Sharpe estimate:   {s.mean():.3f}")
    print(f"Std of Sharpe:     {s.std():.3f}")
    print(f"95% CI: [{np.percentile(s,2.5):.3f}, {np.percentile(s,97.5):.3f}]")

# Your strategy daily returns
np.random.seed(42)
my_returns = np.random.normal(0.05, 1.0, 500)
bootstrap_sharpe(my_returns)
▶ python3 bootstrap_sharpe.py
Sharpe estimate:   0.799
Std of Sharpe:     0.046
95% CI: [0.710, 0.890]
Reading the bootstrap output

Point estimate: Sharpe = 0.799. But the 95% confidence interval is [0.710, 0.890]. You can be 95% confident the true Sharpe is in that range. If the lower bound is still above 0.5, your strategy has meaningful edge. If the CI crosses zero — you have no provable edge and should not trade it live.

Three things to remember from Day 4

The CLT and LLN are not abstract theorems. They set the minimum bar for how much data you need before your trading results mean anything at all.

  • Law of Large Numbers: with only 10 trades your estimated mean has a 11% error. You need hundreds of trades before your backtest converges to the truth.
  • Central Limit Theorem: no matter how ugly your return distribution is, sample means become Normal as n grows. By n=30 skewness drops dramatically — by n=100 it is nearly gone.
  • Standard error = σ / √n. To halve your uncertainty you need 4× more data. This is why quants demand 500+ trade backtests before trusting any strategy.
