Why "Average Return" Lies to You Every Single Time

Every return distribution has 4 numbers that tell the complete story. Most traders only look at one — and it's the most misleading one of all.

NS
Nadun SulochanaAspiring Software Architect · BSc Cyber Security · SLTC Research University

If someone tells you a strategy made 5% average monthly return, would you invest? You probably would. But that number alone tells you almost nothing — and could be hiding catastrophic risk. Here is what you actually need to know.

The 4 Moments That Describe Every Distribution

A probability distribution can be fully characterised by its moments. Each moment tells you something the others cannot. Most risk models use only the first two. The best ones use all four.

1st Moment
Mean (μ) — Expected Return

The centre of gravity of your returns. The direction of your edge. What you expect to make on average across many trades.

E[X] = μ = (1/n) Σ xᵢ
→ Tells you: direction. Doesn't tell you: anything about risk.
2nd Moment
Variance (σ²) — Risk

How spread out your returns are around the mean. The foundation of almost every risk metric — Sharpe ratio, VaR, position sizing.

Var(X) = E[(X − μ)²]
→ Tells you: magnitude of swings. Sharpe ratio stops here.
3rd Moment
Skewness — Direction of Lean

Are your losses bigger than your wins, or vice versa? Negative skew means small steady gains with occasional large crashes. Most stocks have negative skew.

Skew = E[(X − μ)³] / σ³
→ Negative skew = crash risk most traders ignore completely.
4th Moment
Kurtosis — Tail Heaviness

How often do extreme events actually happen? Normal distribution has kurtosis of 3. Real S&P 500 daily returns have kurtosis of 8–12.

Kurt = E[(X − μ)⁴] / σ⁴
→ High kurtosis = black swans happen far more often than models predict.

The Python Code That Calculates All 4

python
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import scipy.stats as stats

# Simulated daily returns — replace with your real data
np.random.seed(42)
r = pd.Series(np.random.normal(0.05, 1.5, 1000))

# All 4 moments in one block
print(f"Mean (μ):      {r.mean():.4f}%")
print(f"Std dev (σ):   {r.std():.4f}%")
print(f"Skewness:      {stats.skew(r):.4f}")
print(f"Kurtosis:      {stats.kurtosis(r):.4f}")

# Geometric mean — the number that actually matters
geo_mean = (np.prod(1 + r/100) ** (1/len(r)) - 1) * 100
print(f"Geometric mean: {geo_mean:.4f}%")

Mean 0.079% — on average, this strategy makes 0.079% per day.

Skewness 0.117 — very slightly right-skewed. Close to symmetric. Safe.
Kurtosis 0.066 — near-zero excess kurtosis. Tails are close to Normal. This is simulated data — real returns would show kurtosis of 5–10.
Geometric mean 0.068% — this is what you actually compound. Always lower than arithmetic mean.

The Number That Breaks Everything: Geometric vs Arithmetic Mean

This is the most important and most misunderstood distinction in all of finance. Average return lies to you. Here is proof.

Strategy A — Looks great
+0.46%
Arithmetic mean
Month 1: +10.0%
Month 2: −9.09%
Average = (10 − 9.09) / 2 = +0.46%

But LKR 100 → LKR 110 → LKR 100
Geometric mean = 0.00%
You went absolutely nowhere.
Strategy B — Actually great
+1.00%
Both means agree
Every month: +1.0%
Average = +1.0%

LKR 100 × 1.01¹² = LKR 112.68
Geometric mean = +1.00%
Actually compounding every month.
The core insight

Strategy A has a higher arithmetic mean (+0.46%) but zero compound growth. Strategy B has the same arithmetic mean (+1.0%) AND the same geometric mean. Arithmetic mean tells you the average of individual period returns. Geometric mean tells you your actual wealth growth. You bank the geometric mean. Always.

Why the Sharpe Ratio Is Incomplete

The Sharpe ratio is the most widely used performance metric in finance. It uses only the 1st and 2nd moments — mean and standard deviation. It is completely blind to skewness and kurtosis.

Moment What it measures Sharpe uses it? Why it matters
1st — Mean Expected return ✓ Yes Direction of edge
2nd — Variance Volatility / risk ✓ Yes Size of swings
3rd — Skewness Crash vs rally asymmetry ✗ No Are losses bigger than gains?
4th — Kurtosis Fat tails / black swans ✗ No How often do crashes happen?
The dangerous conclusion

A strategy can have a Sharpe ratio of 2.0, look completely safe on paper, yet have strong negative skewness and excess kurtosis of 8. It will perform smoothly for months — then lose a year of gains in a single week. The Sharpe ratio never warned you. The 3rd and 4th moments would have.

What to Do With This in Practice

Every time you evaluate a strategy — yours or someone else's — run all four moments. Here is a simple function to do it in one line:

python
import pandas as pd
import scipy.stats as stats
import numpy as np

def describe_returns(r: pd.Series):
    geo = (np.prod(1 + r/100) ** (1/len(r)) - 1) * 100
    sharpe = r.mean() / r.std() * np.sqrt(252)
    return {
        "arithmetic_mean": round(r.mean(), 4),
        "geometric_mean":  round(geo, 4),
        "std_dev":         round(r.std(), 4),
        "skewness":        round(stats.skew(r), 4),
        "kurtosis":        round(stats.kurtosis(r), 4),
        "ann_sharpe":      round(sharpe, 4),
    }

# Use it on any return series
print(describe_returns(your_returns))
Three things to remember from Day 2

Four numbers describe any return distribution completely. Most traders only look at one. The other three are where the real risk hides.

  • Arithmetic mean lies. Geometric mean is what you actually compound and bank. Always check both.
  • The Sharpe ratio uses only 2 of the 4 moments. A high Sharpe can still blow up if skewness is negative and kurtosis is high.
  • Always run all 4 moments on any strategy before trusting it. Mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis — together they tell the truth.
